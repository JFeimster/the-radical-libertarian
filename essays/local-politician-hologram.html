Why Your Local Politician Is A Hologram

A rant about power, theater, bureaucracy, and the machinery hiding behind local political stagecraft.

The hologram has yard signs.

Your local politician may be a real human being with a real haircut and a real appetite for ribbon cuttings. But the role often functions like a hologram: visible, emotionally charged, and mostly projected by machinery you cannot see from the folding chairs.

People imagine local politics as direct democracy in comfortable shoes. In practice, it often becomes a maze of staff recommendations, consultant language, grant incentives, state mandates, procurement rules, stakeholder theater, and meetings scheduled at times when normal people are doing normal-people survival rituals.

The script is older than the candidate.

The faces change. The nouns change. The moral panic gets a seasonal wardrobe. But the governing script is stubbornly familiar: identify crisis, expand scope, increase budget, centralize decision-making, celebrate process, blame constraints, repeat.

The politician becomes the mascot for a machine that was already moving.

Why the hologram works.

Local politics runs on boredom. The boring parts are where the bodies are buried: zoning language, budget footnotes, committee referrals, consent agendas, procurement preferences, emergency authorities, and staff reports written in fluent fog.

The public gets a speech. The machine gets a mechanism.

What to do instead of yelling at the projection.

  • Read meeting agendas before the meeting.
  • Track budgets, not slogans.
  • Learn who actually drafts the policy.
  • Follow procurement and grants.
  • Ask boring questions in public.
  • Build local media and archive everything.
The public gets a speech. The machine gets a mechanism.

Daylight is an offensive weapon.

The solution is not hero worship with a different yard sign. The solution is local literacy, public memory, organized attention, and enough people willing to sit through the boring parts without falling asleep into the tax base.
